How to Start a Mobile Detailing Business: The Complete 2026 Guide

March 2026 · 12 min read

Mobile detailing is one of the most accessible businesses you can start in the auto care industry. Low overhead, flexible hours, and a client base that's growing every year. But "accessible" doesn't mean "easy" — and the difference between a detailer who's booked out three weeks and one who's struggling to fill a Tuesday usually comes down to how they set things up from the start.

This guide walks you through everything you need to go from zero to operational — equipment, pricing, legal basics, marketing, and the systems that keep you profitable as you grow.

What You Actually Need to Get Started

Let's start with the uncomfortable truth: you don't need a $50,000 trailer setup to launch. The detailers who spend six months researching the "perfect" equipment list before doing a single car are usually the ones who never launch at all. Start lean, reinvest profits into better gear, and upgrade as demand justifies it.

Essential Equipment (Day One)

Your bare minimum to start doing quality work includes a reliable pressure washer (electric is fine to start — 1,700-2,000 PSI), a wet/dry vacuum, a quality dual-action polisher, a portable water tank if you don't have reliable water access at client locations, microfiber towels (buy more than you think you need), wash mitts, buckets with grit guards, and a basic selection of chemicals — a good car soap, all-purpose cleaner, glass cleaner, tire dressing, and a spray wax or sealant.

Plan to spend somewhere between $2,000 and $5,000 on your initial setup. You can absolutely start at the lower end and still deliver professional results. The products matter more than the brand names — focus on proper technique and you'll outperform someone with a $20,000 trailer and sloppy habits.

Your Vehicle

You need something that can carry your equipment reliably. A truck, SUV, or van all work. You don't need a wrapped, custom-outfitted van on day one — that's a marketing upgrade, not an operational requirement. Make sure your vehicle is clean, presentable, and organized. Your car is the first impression before you even shake a hand.

Legal and Business Setup

Don't skip this part. It's not glamorous, but getting your business structure right from the beginning saves you headaches (and money) down the road.

Register your business — an LLC is the most common structure for mobile detailers, and it protects your personal assets. Get your EIN from the IRS (free, takes 5 minutes online). Check your local requirements for business licenses and permits — some cities require a specific mobile vendor permit or water runoff compliance. Get insurance. General liability at minimum, and consider adding a garagekeepers policy that covers damage to vehicles in your care.

Open a separate business bank account. This is non-negotiable. Mixing personal and business finances is one of the fastest ways to lose track of your profitability and create a nightmare at tax time.

Setting Your Prices

Pricing is where most new detailers leave the most money on the table. We've written a full article on this topic (link below), but here's the high-level framework:

Don't price based on what the guy down the street charges. Price based on your costs (products, drive time, labor), your target hourly rate, and the value you're delivering. A full interior detail that takes you three hours and uses $15 in product should not be priced at $75 just because someone on Facebook is advertising it for that.

Start by calculating your true cost per job, add your desired hourly rate, and build in a margin for overhead and profit. Most successful mobile detailers in 2026 are charging $150-300 for a full detail on a standard sedan, with premium services like ceramic coating and paint correction going significantly higher.

Pro Tip: Create service packages (Good / Better / Best) rather than a la carte pricing. Packages make it easier for customers to choose, and most people pick the middle option — which should be your most profitable service.

Getting Your First Customers

Your first 20 customers will almost certainly come from your personal network. Tell everyone you know — friends, family, coworkers, neighbors. Post on your personal social media. Offer a launch special (not a deep discount — a slight introductory rate or a free add-on service) to generate momentum and reviews.

After your network, the next wave comes from local visibility. Get your Google Business Profile set up immediately — this is how people searching "mobile detailer near me" find you. Post before-and-after photos consistently on Instagram and Facebook. Join local community groups and car enthusiast pages. Ask every happy customer for a Google review — this is the single most impactful thing you can do for long-term growth.

Build a Professional Website

You need a website. Not a Facebook page — an actual website with your services, pricing, contact info, and a booking system. It doesn't have to cost you anything. A professional, mobile-optimized site gives you credibility that a social media page simply can't match, and it's where your Google search traffic will land.

Systems That Keep You Profitable

Here's what separates the detailers who are still around in year three from the ones who burned out in year one: systems.

Track every dollar coming in and going out. Know your cost per job. Know your average revenue per customer. Know your rebooking rate. If you can't answer "what was my profit margin last month?" within 30 seconds, you're running on gut feeling — and gut feeling doesn't scale.

Set up a simple CRM to track your customers, their vehicles, what services you've done, and when they're due for their next appointment. Automate your follow-ups — a text reminder 90 days after a detail that says "Hey, your car's probably due for some love" will generate more repeat business than any Instagram post.

Invest in a booking system. The more friction you remove from the scheduling process, the more appointments you'll book. If someone has to call you, wait for a callback, and negotiate a time via text, you've already lost a percentage of potential customers.

Scaling Beyond Solo

Once you're consistently booked out and turning away work, you've got a decision to make: raise prices, hire someone, or both. Most successful mobile detailing businesses hit this inflection point within 6-12 months.

Before hiring, raise your prices first. You'd be surprised how much room you have. If you're booked three weeks out, you're underpriced. Raise rates 15-20%, see what happens. You'll lose some price-sensitive customers and gain margin on everyone else — and often end up making more money working fewer hours.

When you do hire, start with one employee doing the services you've systematized and documented. Your job shifts from doing details to managing quality, marketing, and customer relationships. This is where having proper business management tools becomes critical — you can't track multiple technicians, vehicles, and customer accounts on a spreadsheet.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Underpricing to "build a client base." You'll build a client base of people who only value you for being cheap. When you eventually raise prices (and you will have to), they'll leave. Start at a fair price and attract clients who value quality.

Spending too much on equipment before you have revenue. That $800 steamer is nice, but it's not going to get you customers. Spend your money on things that generate bookings first — website, business cards, Google Business Profile, a small ad budget.

Not tracking your numbers. Revenue is not profit. A $300 detail that takes you 5 hours, costs $40 in product, and requires 45 minutes of drive time each way is actually a $33/hour job. Know your numbers.

Ignoring the business side. You got into this because you love making cars look amazing. That's great. But the detailers who survive long-term are the ones who also learn to love the business — marketing, customer service, financial management, and operations.

The Bottom Line

Starting a mobile detailing business in 2026 is a genuinely viable path. The demand is there, the barriers to entry are reasonable, and the earning potential is real. But treat it like a business from day one — not a side hustle, not a hobby that makes money. Set up your structure, price your work fairly, market yourself consistently, and build the systems that let you scale.

The detailers who are booked out weeks in advance aren't necessarily better at polishing paint than you are. They're better at running a business. And that's a skill you can learn.
